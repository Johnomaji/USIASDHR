## Purpose

The purpose of this training is to equip RBM Autism Foundation staff with practical, evidence-based skills for supporting individuals on the autism spectrum within a daycare and educational setting.

This training is not intended to certify staff as Speech-Language Pathologists, Occupational Therapists, Behaviour Analysts, or other licensed professionals. Rather, it is designed to strengthen staff knowledge, confidence, and day-to-day support strategies for individuals on the autism spectrum.

The training will focus on communication support, behaviour support, sensory needs, independence, classroom management, and family collaboration.

## Training Goals

By the end of the training, staff will be able to:

- Demonstrate a foundational understanding of Autism Spectrum Disorder.
- Identify common communication differences in individuals on the autism spectrum.
- Support communication using visual and interaction-based strategies.
- Understand behaviour as a form of communication.
- Implement positive behaviour support techniques.
- Recognise sensory needs and provide appropriate support.
- Promote independence during daily activities.
- Create a structured and autism-friendly environment.
- Communicate effectively with families and caregivers.

## Training Format

- **Duration:** 4 Days
- **Session Length:** 4–6 Hours Daily
- **Training Methods:** Interactive Presentation | Group Discussion | Role Play | Demonstrations | Case Studies | Practical Activities

Following completion of the training, staff will possess practical knowledge and skills that can immediately improve their support of individuals on the autism spectrum attending RBM Autism Foundation.

The training will strengthen staff confidence, improve communication support, promote positive behaviour practices, and enhance the overall quality of care provided to individuals and families.

# DAY ONE
## Understanding Neurodivergence, Autism, and Communication

## MODULE 1: UNDERSTANDING NEURODIVERGENCE & AUTISM SPECTRUM DISORDER

**Rationale:** To effectively support individuals on the autism spectrum, staff must first understand that autism exists within a broader landscape of neurological difference. This module builds that foundation, ensuring staff approach every individual with an informed, strengths-based, and inclusive perspective.

**Learning Outcome:** Staff will develop a foundational understanding of neurodivergence as a broad concept, recognise that autism is one of many neurological differences, and build a strengths-based framework for supporting individuals on the autism spectrum specifically.

### 1. What is Neurodivergence?

**Definition**

- Neurodivergence refers to neurological development that differs from what is considered typical — it is a natural part of human variation, not a defect
- Coined by sociologist Judy Singer in the 1990s, the term shifted the conversation from deficit to difference

**The Neurodiversity Movement**

- Neurodiversity recognises that all brains develop differently — some people think, learn, and process the world in ways that diverge from the majority
- This perspective does not dismiss the real challenges neurodivergent individuals face, but reframes those challenges within a social and environmental context

### 2. Common Neurodivergent Conditions

**Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)**
- Affects communication, social interaction, sensory processing, and behaviour — the primary focus of this training

**ADHD (Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder)**
- Characterised by difficulties sustaining attention, impulse control, and/or hyperactivity — often co-occurs with autism

**Dyslexia**
- A specific learning difference affecting reading, writing, and spelling — not related to intelligence

**Dyspraxia / Developmental Coordination Disorder (DCD)**
- Affects motor planning, coordination, and organisation of movement — can impact writing, dressing, and physical activities

**Dyscalculia**
- A specific learning difference affecting numerical processing, understanding of maths concepts, and number sense

**Tourette Syndrome**
- Characterised by repetitive, involuntary movements or vocalisations called tics — often co-occurs with ADHD and OCD

**Sensory Processing Disorder (SPD)**
- Difficulty organising and responding to sensory information — may present as over- or under-sensitivity across one or more senses

### 3. How Neurodivergent Conditions Overlap

- It is very common for an individual to have more than one neurodivergent profile — for example, autism and ADHD co-occur in approximately 50–70% of cases
- Overlapping presentations mean that strategies effective for one condition often benefit others — sensory supports, visual schedules, and clear routines help across many neurodivergent profiles
- Avoid assuming a single label explains everything you observe — always look at the whole individual

### 4. What is Autism Spectrum Disorder?

**Definition and Prevalence**

- Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) is a neurodevelopmental condition characterised by differences in social communication, sensory processing, and patterns of behaviour
- Prevalence is estimated at approximately 1 in 100 individuals globally; rates in Nigeria are less formally documented but growing awareness is driving more diagnoses

**Autism as a Spectrum**

- No two individuals on the autism spectrum are the same — the spectrum refers to the wide range of presentations, strengths, and support needs
- 'Spectrum' does not mean a line from mild to severe — it refers to variability across multiple dimensions: communication, sensory, social, motor, and cognitive

**Core Characteristics**

- Differences in social communication: difficulty with reciprocal conversation, reading social cues, or understanding unspoken rules
- Restricted or repetitive patterns of behaviour: strong preferences for routines, special interests, and repetitive movements (stimming)
- Sensory differences: over- or under-sensitivity to sound, light, texture, smell, taste, movement, or temperature

### 5. Strengths, Myths, and the Nigerian Context

**Strengths and Abilities**

- Many individuals on the autism spectrum demonstrate exceptional attention to detail, deep focus on areas of interest, strong visual thinking, honesty, and loyalty
- A strengths-based approach begins by asking 'what can this individual do?' before 'what can't they do?'

**Common Myths**

- **Myth:** Autism is caused by vaccines — **Fact:** This claim has been thoroughly disproven by decades of research
- **Myth:** Individuals on the autism spectrum cannot feel or show affection — **Fact:** They feel deeply; they may express affection differently
- **Myth:** All individuals on the autism spectrum have intellectual disability — **Fact:** Autism occurs across the full range of intellectual ability
- **Myth:** Autism can be cured — **Fact:** Autism is a lifelong neurological difference, not a disease; support focuses on quality of life, not cure

**Nigerian Context**

- In Nigeria, autism is sometimes misattributed to spiritual causes, family wrongdoing, or witchcraft — staff must be equipped to respectfully provide accurate information to families
- Limited diagnostic services, cultural stigma, and lack of specialist training remain significant barriers — this training is part of addressing that gap

## MODULE 2: COMMUNICATION AND LANGUAGE SUPPORT

**Rationale:** Communication is at the heart of everything — learning, relationships, behaviour, and wellbeing. Individuals on the autism spectrum communicate differently, not less. Understanding how they communicate and adapting our responses is one of the most powerful things a support worker can do.

**Learning Outcome:** Staff will learn practical strategies to encourage communication, reduce communication breakdowns, and support all individuals on the autism spectrum to be heard — regardless of how they communicate.

### 1. How Individuals on the Autism Spectrum Communicate

**Communication is More Than Speech**

- Communication includes gestures, facial expressions, body language, eye gaze, vocalisation, pictures, symbols, devices, and behaviour
- Never assume an individual who does not speak has nothing to say — non-speaking individuals on the autism spectrum often have rich inner lives and benefit enormously from alternative communication methods

**Range of Communication Profiles**

- Some individuals on the autism spectrum speak fluently but struggle with the social rules of conversation: turn-taking, staying on topic, reading listener cues
- Others use single words or short phrases; some may be minimally verbal or non-speaking

### 2. Verbal and Non-Verbal Communication

**Verbal Communication Differences**

- Literal interpretation of language: idioms, sarcasm, and indirect requests may be confusing — use plain, direct language
- Prosody differences: some individuals on the autism spectrum speak in an unusual rhythm, pitch, or volume — this is not rudeness, it is neurological

**Non-Verbal Communication**

- Reduced or atypical eye contact is common — do not insist on it; it can be uncomfortable and cognitively demanding
- Gestures and pointing may develop later or differently — respond to whatever communication attempt the individual makes

### 3. Echolalia

**What is Echolalia?**

- Echolalia is the repetition of words or phrases — either immediately after hearing them (immediate) or later (delayed)
- Examples: repeating a question back, quoting from a favourite TV programme, or reciting scripts from memory

**Echolalia is Communicative**

- Echolalia is not meaningless — it is often the individual's best available communication tool and should be responded to, not ignored or corrected
- Over time, echolalia often evolves into more flexible language with consistent, supportive interaction

### 4. Visual Supports and AAC

**Visual Supports**

- Pictures, symbols, objects, and written words reduce reliance on processing spoken language in real time — they are permanent and can be referred back to
- Visual supports include: choice boards, now-next boards, visual schedules, emotion cards, and visual instructions for tasks

**Introduction to AAC**

- Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC) includes any tool that supplements or replaces speech: picture exchange (PECS), communication books, speech-generating devices, and apps
- AAC does not prevent speech development — research consistently shows it supports it
- All individuals who cannot reliably communicate their needs deserve access to an AAC system

### 5. Supporting Communication in Daily Routines

- Embed communication opportunities throughout every routine: mealtimes, dressing, play, and transitions are all communication learning moments
- Use 'expectant pausing' — pause and look at the individual with expectation during routines to create space for communication attempts
- Avoid yes/no questions when the individual can express more — open-ended prompts with visual support invite richer communication
- Respond to every communication attempt, however small — this builds confidence and motivation to communicate more

## MODULE 3: BUILDING POSITIVE RELATIONSHIPS

**Rationale:** An individual on the autism spectrum who trusts their support worker is more likely to engage, learn, attempt new things, and tolerate challenges. Positive relationships are not a 'nice extra' — they are the single most powerful therapeutic and educational tool a support worker has.

**Learning Outcome:** Staff will learn how building trust and following the individual's lead creates the relational foundation for participation, learning, and positive behaviour.

### 1. Building Trust

**Why Trust Matters Most**

- Many individuals on the autism spectrum have experienced repeated frustration, misunderstanding, or forced compliance — trust must be actively built, not assumed
- Trust is built through consistency, predictability, following through on what you say, and never using the relationship as leverage for compliance

**Practical Trust-Building Strategies**

- Be consistent: same greetings, same tone, same expectations every day
- Keep promises — if you say 'after this we will go outside,' always follow through
- Never use withdrawal of the relationship as a consequence — this damages the foundation you are building

### 2. Following the Individual's Lead

**What It Means**

- Following the individual's lead means observing what they are interested in, joining them in it, and building interaction from there — rather than directing the individual toward your agenda
- This is not permissiveness — it is a strategic approach to building engagement and communication

**How to Do It**

- Observe before interacting: watch what the individual does, touches, looks at, or vocalises about
- Join the activity at their level — if they are lining up cars, sit beside them and do the same
- Comment on what they are doing rather than asking questions: 'You're putting the red one here' rather than 'What colour is that?'

### 3. Pairing Strategies

**What is Pairing?**

- Pairing is the process of associating yourself with things the individual already finds positive — you become a predictor of good things
- Before introducing any demands or teaching, spend time simply being present during preferred activities with no expectations attached

**How Long Does Pairing Take?**

- Pairing is not a one-session activity — with individuals who have had difficult experiences of adults, it may take days or weeks
- Signs of successful pairing: the individual seeks you out, tolerates your proximity, makes eye contact, or offers you objects

### 4. Encouraging Engagement Through Play

**Play as the Language of Childhood**

- For individuals on the autism spectrum, play may look different — solitary, repetitive, sensory-based, or focused on a specific interest — and all of these are valid
- Meet the individual in their play before trying to expand or redirect it

**Expanding Play Gradually**

- Once you have joined and are accepted in the individual's play, introduce one small variation at a time — a new character, a new sound, a new role
- Celebrate any reciprocal play moment, however brief — a shared look, a turn-take, a smile
- Use the individual's special interests as a bridge to teach colours, counting, turn-taking, and requesting

# DAY TWO
## Behaviour Support and Sensory Needs

## MODULE 4: UNDERSTANDING BEHAVIOUR

**Rationale:** Every behaviour has a reason. When we understand why a behaviour is occurring — rather than simply reacting to what it looks like — we can respond in ways that actually help. This module is the foundation for all behaviour support work.

**Learning Outcome:** Staff will understand how to analyse behaviour functionally and respond in ways that address the underlying need rather than just the surface presentation.

### 1. Behaviour as Communication

**The Core Principle**

- All behaviour is communication — particularly for individuals on the autism spectrum who may not have reliable verbal language to express their needs, feelings, or overwhelm
- When an individual hits, runs, screams, or shuts down, they are telling us something — our job is to figure out what

**Common Messages Behind Behaviour**

- 'I am overwhelmed by something in my environment'
- 'I want something I cannot ask for'
- 'I do not want to do this and I have no other way to say so'
- 'I am in pain, hungry, tired, or unwell'
- 'I am scared and I do not feel safe right now'

### 2. Why Behaviour Occurs: The Four Functions

**SEAT — A Useful Framework**

- **Social Attention:** the behaviour gets a response from people
- **Escape / Avoidance:** the behaviour allows the individual to get out of something they find difficult, painful, or overwhelming
- **Access to Tangibles:** the behaviour results in getting a preferred item, activity, or place
- **Automatic / Sensory:** the behaviour feels good in itself — it is self-reinforcing regardless of what anyone else does

**Why This Matters**

- The same behaviour in two different individuals may have completely different functions — treating it the same way in every individual will fail
- Function-based responses are essential for effective, compassionate behaviour support

### 3. Identifying Triggers

**Internal Triggers**

- Hunger, thirst, pain, fatigue, illness, anxiety, and sensory overload are common internal triggers
- Track patterns: does behaviour occur at a consistent time of day, before or after transitions, or when the environment changes?

**External Triggers**

- Specific demands, noise levels, social demands, changes to routine, or presence of particular people or materials
- Identifying triggers allows staff to prevent behaviour before it escalates — prevention is always more effective than reaction

### 4. The ABC Model

**Antecedent → Behaviour → Consequence**

- **Antecedent:** what happened immediately before the behaviour? (the trigger or setting event)
- **Behaviour:** what exactly did the individual do? (describe objectively — not 'had a tantrum' but 'screamed for 3 minutes and lay on the floor')
- **Consequence:** what happened immediately after? (what did the individual get or avoid as a result?)

**Using ABC Data**

- Collecting ABC observations over time reveals patterns that identify the function of behaviour
- ABC data should be recorded on the same day — memory is unreliable and descriptions must be specific and objective

### 5. Responding Appropriately

- Stay calm — a regulated adult is the most powerful co-regulator for a dysregulated individual
- Reduce demands during escalation — this is not rewarding behaviour, it is reducing fuel on the fire
- Never use physical restraint unless trained and unless there is immediate risk of serious harm
- After the behaviour has passed and the individual is calm, reflect on the ABC and plan for prevention next time

## MODULE 5: POSITIVE BEHAVIOUR SUPPORT

**Rationale:** Positive Behaviour Support (PBS) is an evidence-based framework that focuses on teaching new skills and changing environments rather than punishing unwanted behaviour. It is compassionate, effective, and aligned with the rights of individuals on the autism spectrum.

**Learning Outcome:** Staff will learn practical, non-punitive strategies to prevent challenging behaviour, build new skills, and create environments where individuals on the autism spectrum can thrive.

### 1. Reinforcement

**What is Reinforcement?**

- Reinforcement is anything that increases the likelihood of a behaviour occurring again — it is not bribery, it is how all learning works
- Identify reinforcers individually — what motivates one individual may be irrelevant to another

**Using Reinforcement Effectively**

- Be immediate: deliver reinforcement within seconds of the desired behaviour
- Be specific: 'Great sitting — you kept your feet still the whole time' is far more informative than 'Good job'
- Vary reinforcers over time to prevent satiation

### 2. Prevention Strategies

**Environmental Modifications**

- Reduce sensory triggers, provide predictable routines, and ensure the individual always has access to a communication system
- Set the individual up for success before the session begins — check in, preview the schedule, offer a preferred activity first

**Proactive Strategies**

- Offer choices before demands — 'Do you want to start with maths or reading?' gives control and reduces refusal
- Use transition warnings: '5 more minutes, then we will tidy up'

### 3. Visual Schedules

- Visual schedules show the individual what is happening, in what order, and for how long — they replace uncertainty with predictability
- Format depends on the individual's level: objects → photographs → symbols → written words
- Refer to the schedule actively throughout the day — it must be a live tool, not decoration
- Make schedules interactive: the individual ticks off, turns over, or removes each completed item

### 4. Choice-Making and Replacement Behaviours

**Embedding Meaningful Choice**

- Offering genuine choices throughout the day builds autonomy, reduces opposition, and teaches decision-making
- Even small choices matter: which cup, which seat, which activity to start with

**Teaching Replacement Behaviours**

- A replacement behaviour achieves the same function as the challenging behaviour but in an acceptable way — it must be easier for the individual than the original behaviour
- Example: if an individual throws materials to escape a task, teach them to hand a 'break' card instead

### 5. What Not To Do

- Do not use time-out or withdrawal of positive attention as a consequence — these do not teach replacement skills and damage the therapeutic relationship
- Do not threaten, shame, or compare the individual to others
- Do not raise your voice — escalating your own affect escalates the individual's
- Do not wait for a crisis to intervene — proactive support always outperforms reactive management
- Do not assume a strategy will work forever — behaviour support requires ongoing monitoring and adjustment

## MODULE 6: SENSORY PROCESSING AND REGULATION

**Rationale:** The sensory system is central to how individuals on the autism spectrum experience the world. Sensory differences are not quirks or attention-seeking — they are neurological realities that affect learning, behaviour, and wellbeing every moment of every day.

**Learning Outcome:** Staff will learn to identify sensory needs, recognise signs of sensory overload, and create environments and routines that support regulation.

### 1. Sensory Differences in Autism

**The Eight Senses**

- Most people know five senses — but we also have proprioception (body position and force), vestibular (movement and balance), and interoception (internal body signals like hunger and pain)
- Individuals on the autism spectrum can be over-sensitive (hypersensitive), under-sensitive (hyposensitive), or both — across different senses and at different times

**Why Sensory Differences Matter**

- An individual sitting in a classroom with humming fluorescent lights, chattering peers, and scratchy clothing is already using significant cognitive resources just to exist in that environment — before any learning begins
- Sensory support is a prerequisite for access to learning and participation — not optional

### 2. Sensory Seeking Behaviours

- Sensory seeking occurs when the nervous system craves more input — the individual seeks it out
- Examples: spinning, jumping, crashing into objects or people, mouthing items, seeking tight spaces, making loud vocalisations, touching everything
- Sensory seeking is not naughty — the appropriate response is to provide the input safely, not to stop the individual seeking it

### 3. Sensory Avoidance Behaviours

- Sensory avoidance occurs when the nervous system is overwhelmed — the individual does everything possible to escape the input
- Examples: covering ears, refusing to enter certain rooms, removing clothing, meltdowns triggered by specific sounds or textures
- Forced exposure to overwhelming sensory input without support causes trauma — always respect avoidance as a genuine need, not defiance

### 4. Recognising Sensory Overload

**Early Warning Signs**

- Increased stimming, covering ears or eyes, seeking escape, becoming quieter or more withdrawn, flushing, or asking repetitive questions
- Intervene at this stage — before escalation reaches meltdown

**Overload and Meltdown**

- A sensory meltdown is not a tantrum — it is a neurological crisis during which the individual has lost the ability to regulate
- During overload: reduce demands, reduce sensory input, stay calm, give time — do not crowd, question, or instruct

### 5. Calming and Regulation Strategies

**Environmental Modifications**

- Designated quiet areas, access to natural light, reduced clutter, and predictable noise levels lower the baseline sensory load

**Sensory Tools**

- Noise-cancelling headphones, fidget tools, weighted lap pads, chewable tools, and wobble cushions are access supports — not privileges

**Movement as Regulation**

- Proprioceptive input (heavy work, pushing, pulling, carrying) and vestibular input (swinging, rocking) are highly regulating — build them into the daily routine proactively

# DAY THREE
## Independence, Classroom Success, and Family Collaboration

## MODULE 7: TEACHING INDEPENDENCE

**Rationale:** Independence is not achieved all at once — it is built step by step, with the right level of support gradually removed as the individual develops competence and confidence. Every skill learned independently is a lifelong gain.

**Learning Outcome:** Staff will learn practical, structured methods for increasing independence across daily living skills, using task analysis and appropriate prompting strategies.

### 1. Activities of Daily Living (ADLs)

**What Are ADLs?**

- Daily living skills are the tasks a person performs to care for themselves and function in daily life — they form the foundation of independence and quality of life
- For individuals on the autism spectrum, ADLs may present challenges due to sensory sensitivities, motor difficulties, difficulty with sequencing, or rigidity around routine

**Priority Areas**

- Toileting, feeding, dressing, and hygiene are the core areas most directly affecting the individual's dignity, health, and participation in school and community life

### 2. Toileting

- Establish a consistent toileting schedule based on the individual's natural patterns before attempting training
- Use a visual sequence of the steps displayed in the bathroom: pull down → sit → wipe → flush → wash hands
- Address sensory barriers first: the toilet seat texture, the flush sound, and the feeling of clothing removal can all be significant barriers
- Never shame, rush, or punish accidents — approach with complete neutrality

### 3. Feeding and Dressing

**Feeding Skills**

- Break feeding skills into small steps: holding a spoon, scooping, bringing to mouth, chewing — teach one step at a time
- Address sensory preferences in cutlery, cup texture, and plate material — these matter and affect whether the individual will engage with the task

**Dressing Skills**

- Backward chaining is often effective: staff complete most of the task and the individual does the final step, building success from the end of the sequence forward
- Practise dressing skills during low-pressure times — use dolls or clothing activities during play to build motor skills

### 4. Task Analysis

**What is Task Analysis?**

- Task analysis is the process of breaking a complex skill into small, sequential, teachable steps
- Example — handwashing: turn on tap → wet hands → apply soap → rub palms → rub between fingers → rinse → turn off tap → dry hands → dispose of towel

**Why It Works**

- Individuals on the autism spectrum often learn multi-step tasks more successfully when each step is explicitly taught and practised
- Once a task analysis exists, all staff teach the skill in exactly the same sequence — consistency is essential

### 5. Prompting and Fading Support

**Prompt Hierarchy**

- Full physical prompt → Partial physical prompt → Gestural prompt → Model prompt → Verbal prompt → Independent
- Begin with the least intrusive prompt that leads to success — over-prompting creates prompt dependency

**Fading**

- Systematically and gradually reduce the level of support over time as the individual demonstrates competence
- Document progress — movement from a physical to a gestural prompt is real progress, even if independence is not yet reached

## MODULE 8: CREATING AN AUTISM-FRIENDLY ENVIRONMENT

**Rationale:** The environment is either a barrier or a bridge. An autism-friendly environment reduces unnecessary challenge, allowing the individual's energy to go toward learning, connection, and growth rather than survival.

**Learning Outcome:** Staff will understand how thoughtful environmental design and structured routines significantly improve individuals on the autism spectrum's access to learning, reduce anxiety, and prevent challenging behaviour.

### 1. Structured Routines

**Why Predictability Is Regulating**

- Predictability reduces anxiety — when an individual knows what is happening, in what order, and for how long, their nervous system can settle and attend
- Structure does not mean rigidity — it means the individual always knows the overall shape of the day, even when individual activities vary

**Building a Structured Day**

- Consistent arrival, transition, mealtime, and departure routines give the day reliable anchors
- Preview the day at the start of every session using a visual schedule

### 2. Visual Schedules in the Classroom

- Display a whole-day schedule at the individual's eye level using their level of representation: objects, photographs, symbols, or words
- Refer to it actively throughout the day — it must be a live tool, not decoration on the wall
- Individual schedules allow the individual to take ownership and carry predictability with them across spaces

### 3. Transition Supports

- Warn of transitions in advance: '5 minutes, then we finish' — use a visual timer alongside the verbal warning
- Use a physical transition object: the individual carries something from the current activity to the next
- For individuals who find transitions extremely difficult, a visual First-Then board provides a clear and motivating endpoint

### 4. Classroom Organisation

**Reducing Visual Noise**

- Busy, colourful classrooms with many wall displays increase the cognitive and sensory load for individuals on the autism spectrum
- Designate specific areas for specific activities so the individual's nervous system knows what to expect in each space

**Defined Workspaces**

- An individual's workspace should be clear of materials not related to the current task
- Physical boundaries (a mat, a tray, or tape on the desk) help the individual understand where their space begins and ends

### 5. Reducing Environmental Stressors

- **Lighting:** favour natural light; avoid or diffuse harsh fluorescent lighting
- **Sound:** be aware of ambient noise levels — corridor noise, fans, chairs scraping — and reduce where possible
- **Smell:** strong food smells or cleaning products can be highly aversive — minimise where possible
- **Temperature:** individuals on the autism spectrum may not reliably communicate thermal discomfort — check the environment regularly

## MODULE 9: FAMILY COLLABORATION AND PROFESSIONAL PRACTICE

**Rationale:** Families are the constant in an individual's life — support workers come and go. Building strong, respectful, culturally aware partnerships with families amplifies the impact of everything done in the setting and ensures continuity of support across environments.

**Learning Outcome:** Staff will learn strategies for effective, respectful caregiver communication, culturally sensitive practice, and the documentation and teamwork skills that underpin professional support.

### 1. Building Relationships with Families

**Families as Partners, Not Recipients**

- Families know their individual best — their knowledge of triggers, preferences, communication, and history is irreplaceable
- Position yourself as a collaborator: 'What do you notice works at home? What would you like us to try here?'

**First Meetings**

- Begin by asking about the individual's strengths, interests, and what makes them feel safe — not just their challenges and diagnosis

### 2. Respectful and Culturally Sensitive Communication

**Communication Style**

- Be honest, direct, and warm — avoid clinical jargon that distances families from information about their own individual
- Use plain language: 'Trevor finds it hard to cope when the routine changes' rather than clinical terminology

**Cultural Considerations in the Nigerian Context**

- In many Nigerian communities, disability carries significant stigma — families may be navigating shame, grief, and misinformation simultaneously
- Acknowledge this without judgment: 'I know it can be hard when people around you don't understand what autism is'
- Respect religious and spiritual frameworks while providing accurate information
- Involve fathers, grandparents, and extended family where appropriate — in many contexts, multiple adults hold decision-making authority

### 3. Documentation and Observation

**Why Documentation Matters**

- Clear, accurate, and timely records protect the individual, support accountability, and enable the team to track progress and identify patterns
- Good documentation answers: what did the individual do, when, where, with whom, and what happened before and after?

**Principles of Good Recording**

- Write objectively: describe what you saw and heard, not what you think it meant
- Write the same day — memory degrades quickly and inaccurate records are worse than none

### 4. Team Collaboration

**The Multi-Disciplinary Team**

- Support workers are part of a wider team that may include teachers, speech-language pathologists, occupational therapists, behaviour specialists, and medical professionals
- Understand your role clearly — implement recommendations, document observations, and escalate concerns through the right channels

**Communication Within the Team**

- Share relevant observations promptly — something noticed at 9am may be critical to a therapy session at 11am
- Disagreements about an individual's support should be resolved through professional conversation — a unified approach is essential for progress
- Celebrate team wins — shared success builds morale and motivation

## MODULE 10: STRATEGIES FOR EMOTIONAL REGULATION

**Rationale:** Individuals on the autism spectrum often experience intense emotions and may not have the language or tools to manage them. Teaching regulation reduces meltdowns and increases learning readiness.

**Learning Outcome:** Participants will be able to teach, model, and support emotional regulation strategies across home, school, and therapy settings.

### 1. What is Emotional Regulation?

**Definition**

- The ability to manage the intensity and duration of emotional experiences

**Co-regulation vs. Self-regulation**

- **Co-regulation:** an adult helps the individual manage their emotional state through calm presence, tone of voice, and physical support — a necessary precursor to self-regulation
- **Self-regulation:** the individual independently recognises and manages their emotional state — develops gradually and may always require some co-regulatory scaffolding in autism

**Developmental Expectations**

- Individuals on the autism spectrum may require longer timelines and continued co-regulation support — individualise expectations rather than applying typical developmental milestones

### 2. Identify Triggers and Early Warning Signs

**Internal Triggers**

- Hunger, fatigue, illness, and anxiety are common internal drivers of dysregulation
- Individuals may not be able to identify or communicate these states — observe physical signs such as yawning, flushing, or body posture changes

**External Triggers**

- Noise, unexpected transitions, social demands, and sensory overload
- Mapping each individual's personal trigger profile helps caregivers anticipate and prevent escalation

**Emotion Thermometer / Visual Arousal Scale**

- A numbered (1–5) or colour-coded visual tool to track how regulated or dysregulated the individual is feeling
- Use consistently across home, school, and therapy settings to support generalisation

### 3. Create a Quiet Zone / Safe Space

**Environmental Design**

- Low lighting, minimal clutter, soft furnishings, and familiar sensory objects

**Rules for Use**

- The quiet zone is a calming resource — it must never be used as punishment or time-out
- Introduce the space during calm periods so the individual associates it with comfort, not consequence

**Practice Before It Is Needed**

- Role-play using the space during low-demand play sessions so it becomes a familiar, positive option

### 4. Name the Emotion: Building an Emotion Vocabulary

**Visual Supports**

- Emotion cards, Zones of Regulation posters, and social stories provide concrete representations of internal states

**Scripted Language**

- Teach and practise 'I feel ___ because ___' sentence frames through modelling and rehearsal

**Validating Without Immediately Fixing**

- Acknowledge the emotion first: 'I can see you are really frustrated right now — that makes sense'
- Validation reduces escalation more effectively than immediate problem-solving

### 5. Prepare a Sensory Regulation Toolkit

**Sensory Tools**

- Noise-cancelling headphones, fidget or spinning toys, weighted lap pad, chewable jewellery or pencil toppers
- Personalise the toolkit — what regulates one individual may dysregulate another

**Breathing and Movement Tools**

- Bubbles, straw-blowing, and pinwheels support controlled exhalation, activating the parasympathetic nervous system
- Wall pushes, chair push-ups, and heavy work activities provide regulating proprioceptive input

**Creating a 'Calm Down Kit'**

- A portable bag or box containing the individual's personal tools for home and school use
- Involve the individual in selecting items — ownership increases willingness to use the kit independently

## MODULE 11: SITTING TOLERANCE AND STRATEGIES FOR ATTENTION

**Rationale:** Many individuals on the autism spectrum struggle to remain seated for group activities, mealtimes, or tabletop tasks. Building sitting tolerance supports participation and engagement.

**Learning Outcome:** Participants will be able to assess and gradually increase an individual's sitting tolerance using structured, non-coercive strategies.

### 1. What is Sitting Tolerance?

**Definition and Why It Matters**

- The ability to remain seated for a functional period during learning, therapy, mealtimes, or social activities

**'Can't Sit' vs. 'Won't Sit'**

- Sensory or motor-based difficulty must be distinguished from behavioural avoidance before strategies are selected
- Both require compassionate, non-punitive responses — coercion increases anxiety and avoidance

**Baseline Assessment**

- Observe and record how long the individual can sit before escape, protest, or significant distraction

### 2. Environmental and Sensory Supports

**Adaptive Seating Options**

- Wobble cushion, therapy ball, standing desk, floor seating with back support, or bean bag

**Reducing Distractions**

- Visual boundaries, minimal wall décor, and quiet working corners reduce environmental triggers for movement

**Movement Breaks**

- Schedule 'wiggle time' immediately before seated tasks
- Movement breaks are a neurological necessity — not a reward or privilege

### 3. Structured Sitting Programmes

**Graduated Exposure**

- Begin at the individual's current baseline and increase sitting time by small, manageable increments
- Use a visual timer so the individual can see time passing — predictability reduces anxiety

**First-Then Boards**

- 'First sit for [time], Then [preferred activity]' creates a clear, motivating endpoint

**Reinforcement Systems**

- Identify individually meaningful reinforcers — reinforce the behaviour of sitting, not compliance

### 4. Embedding Choice and Engagement

**Individual-Led Choices**

- Allowing the individual to choose their seat, activity, or fidget tool gives a sense of control that reduces opposition

**High-Interest Tasks**

- Using topics and materials the individual is motivated by increases time on task naturally

**Pairing Strategy**

- An adult sits beside the individual in a calm, playful, low-demand way — presence co-regulates and motivates

### 5. When to Refer and Safety Considerations

**Red Flags Requiring Referral**

- Reports of pain when sitting, visible postural asymmetry, constant distressed escape attempts, or regression in previously acquired sitting ability

**Collaborative Team Approach**

- **Occupational Therapist:** core strength, motor planning, and specialised seating equipment
- **Physiotherapist:** postural support, movement needs, and equipment assessment
- **Psychologist or Behaviour Specialist:** where avoidance is primarily anxiety- or behaviour-based

## MODULE 12: PICKY EATING, FOOD AVERSION & FRUIT/VEGETABLE REFUSAL

**Rationale:** Food selectivity is common in autism and impacts nutritional health, family mealtimes, and school participation. This module equips participants with non-forceful, evidence-informed, sensory-aware feeding strategies.

**Learning Outcome:** Participants will understand the reasons behind picky eating in autism and apply step-based, sensory-informed feeding strategies to expand food acceptance without pressure.

### 1. What is Picky Eating in Autism?

**Contributing Factors**

- Sensory hypersensitivity to texture, smell, taste, temperature, or visual appearance of food
- Rigid eating patterns and strong need for sameness
- Behavioural avoidance learned from past negative or forced mealtime experiences
- Medical factors: gastro-oesophageal reflux, oral motor difficulties, or constipation

**Preference vs. Aversion vs. ARFID**

- **Preference:** a limited but stable repertoire of accepted foods
- **Aversion:** strong sensory or anxiety-based rejection with distress at the sight or proximity of the food
- **ARFID:** extreme restriction impacting nutrition and development — requires specialist referral

**When to Refer**

- **Paediatrician:** if weight loss, nutritional deficiency, or medical symptoms are present
- **Dietitian:** for nutritional analysis and supplement guidance
- **Speech-Language Pathologist:** if oral motor difficulties, swallowing concerns, or ARFID-level restriction are suspected

### 2. Understanding Sensory Profiles of Food

**Texture**

- Crunchy vs. mushy, smooth vs. lumpy, wet vs. dry — the same food prepared differently may be accepted or rejected

**Other Sensory Dimensions**

- Taste, smell, temperature, and visual appearance all matter — mixed foods on the same plate can be a significant barrier

**Food Chaining**

- Expanding gradually from an accepted food to similar foods by changing one characteristic at a time

### 3. The Food Exploration Ladder: No Force, No Pressure

**Step-by-Step Exposure Hierarchy**

- Step 1 — Look at the food in the environment
- Step 2 — Touch the food (with a utensil first, then fingers)
- Step 3 — Smell the food
- Step 4 — Kiss or bring the food to the lips
- Step 5 — Lick the food
- Step 6 — Take a small bite
- Step 7 — Chew
- Step 8 — Swallow

**Important:** Progress at the individual's pace. Force or pressure at any stage can cause regression and increased aversion.

### 4. Addressing Fruit and Vegetable Avoidance

**Presentation Variations**

- Frozen, smoothies, dehydrated or baked vegetable chips, roasted vs. raw — changing form changes texture, smell, and appearance

**Pairing Strategies**

- Introduce new foods alongside a preferred dip or texture

**Repeated Neutral Exposure**

- Research suggests 10–15+ exposures may be needed before acceptance — consistency without pressure is key

### 5. Creating Positive Mealtime Environments

**Predictable Routines**

- Visual menus, consistent seating, and 5-minute transition warnings before meals reduce anticipatory anxiety

**Avoiding Power Struggles: The Division of Responsibility Model**

- **Caregiver's role:** decide what is served, when, and where
- **Individual's role:** decide whether to eat and how much

**Tracking and Knowing When to Seek Help**

- Seek professional support when the accepted food repertoire is fewer than 20 foods, or when anxiety around eating is significantly impacting family life

## MODULE 13: AUTISM & TACTILE TEXTURE SENSITIVITY

**Rationale:** Tactile defensiveness can block dressing, grooming, writing, play, and social participation. Understanding the neurological basis of tactile sensitivity prevents the individual's responses from being mislabelled as 'stubborn' or 'naughty.'

**Learning Outcome:** Trainees and participants will identify tactile triggers and implement desensitisation and accommodation strategies in collaboration with occupational therapy.

### 1. Understanding Tactile Sensitivity in Autism

**Tactile Defensiveness vs. Tactile Seeking**

- **Tactile defensiveness:** the nervous system perceives ordinary touch as threatening or painful — avoidance and distress are protective responses
- **Tactile seeking:** the individual craves touch input and actively seeks it — often misread as disruptive behaviour

**Neurological Basis**

- In tactile defensiveness, the protective touch pathway over-activates — this is a neurological difference, not wilfulness

### 2. Common Tactile Triggers

**Clothing**

- Tags, thick seams, tight waistbands, and certain synthetic fabrics

**Wet, Sticky, or Messy Hands**

- Glue, paint, sand, slime, playdough, soil, and finger foods that leave residue

**Personal Care and Hygiene**

- Toothbrush bristles, haircuts, nail cutting, face-washing, and application of sunscreen or creams

### 3. Desensitisation and Gradual Exposure Strategies

**Brushing Protocol and Deep Pressure**

- The Wilbarger Brushing Protocol must be taught and supervised by a trained Occupational Therapist
- Deep pressure is generally more tolerable than light touch for individuals with tactile defensiveness

**Play-Based Exposure Progression**

- Dry (rice, pasta, sand) → damp (wet sand, kinetic sand) → messy (shaving foam, paint) — always allow the individual to control pace and exit

**Respecting 'No'**

- Forced tactile exposure increases trauma — never force touch

### 4. Accommodations for Daily Life

**Clothing Adaptations**

- Tagless clothing, seamless socks, soft jersey fabrics — allow a preferred outfit to be worn repeatedly if it supports daily function

**Hygiene Adaptations**

- Wet wipes as an alternative to handwashing; electric toothbrush for more predictable input; trimming nails after a bath

**Classroom Accommodations**

- Allow gloves during art; offer alternative writing tools; permit private handwashing

### 5. Building Tolerance Through Play

**Tactile Play Menu (Graduated Progression)**

- **Level 1 — Dry:** rice bins, dried pasta, sand, buttons, fabric swatches
- **Level 2 — Damp:** wet sand, damp sponges, water play with objects
- **Level 3 — Messy:** shaving foam, finger paint, playdough, kinetic sand — introduce last and at the individual's pace

**Embedding into Preferred Activities**

- If the individual loves dinosaurs, hide them in the sensory bin — motivation supports tactile tolerance naturally

## MODULE 14: SCREENTIME MANAGEMENT & AUDIOVISUAL INPUT

**Rationale:** Unmanaged screentime and audiovisual input can impact attention, regulation, sleep, communication, and social development. Understanding how screens affect the nervous system of individuals on the autism spectrum helps caregivers move from restriction and conflict to intentional, supportive management that protects development while respecting the individual's needs.

**Learning Outcome:** Trainees and participants will identify the effects of screentime and audiovisual input, and apply duration, content, and environmental management techniques in collaboration with caregivers and therapists.

### 1. Understanding Screentime in Autism

**What is Screentime?**

- **Screentime:** time spent engaging with phones, tablets, TV, computers, and gaming devices
- **Audiovisual Input:** the combined effect of visual images, light, sound, and pacing on the nervous system

**Why It Matters for Individuals on the Autism Spectrum**

- Screens provide predictable, controllable stimulation that can be regulating
- Overuse can reduce opportunities for co-regulation, play, communication, and sleep
- Sensory factors: flicker, brightness, sound volume, and fast cuts can be overwhelming or highly engaging

### 2. Common Challenges Related to Screentime

**Regulation & Transition**

- Difficulty stopping or transitioning away from screens due to hyperfocus
- Increased irritability or meltdown when screens are removed

**Content & Input**

- Preference for repetitive videos, specific sounds, or fast-paced content
- Passive viewing replacing interactive communication and play

**Physiological Effects**

- Disrupted sleep patterns from blue light and late viewing
- Reduced tolerance for other sensory inputs after prolonged exposure

### 3. Management Techniques & Strategies

**Environmental Management**

- Create screen-free zones: bedrooms, dining areas, and during social activities
- Use blue-light filters, lower brightness, and control volume

**Behavioural Strategies**

- Visual schedules and timers to show when screentime starts and ends
- 'First-Then' boards: First activity, Then screentime
- Co-viewing to build connection and model joint attention

**Graduated Reduction**

- Replace, don't just remove: offer preferred non-screen alternatives before the transition
- Use screentime as a reinforcer after completing tasks or routines

### 4. Duration & Healthy Guidelines

**Recommended Duration**

- Toddlers 18–24 months: minimal, only high-quality co-viewed content
- Preschool 2–5 years: 1 hour per day maximum, co-viewed when possible
- School-age 6+ years: consistent limits, balanced with sleep, activity, and social time

**Quality Over Quantity**

- Prioritise interactive, educational, and co-regulated content
- Monitor for signs of overload: zoning out, irritability, reduced eye contact, sleep issues

**Family Collaboration**

- Agree on household rules and model balanced use
- Track patterns with caregivers to identify triggers and effective alternatives

### 5. Building Balanced Media Habits

**Screen Alternatives Menu**

- **Level 1 — Low stimulation:** audiobooks, music, puzzles, drawing
- **Level 2 — Active play:** movement games, sensory bins, outdoor play
- **Level 3 — Social connection:** turn-taking games, joint crafts, conversation

**Embedding into Preferred Interests**

- If the individual loves trains, use train videos to teach turn-taking, then transition to playing with toy trains

**Respecting Autonomy**

- Forced abrupt removal increases distress — always warn, use timers, and validate feelings

## MODULE 15: OTHER CONCEPTS ASSOCIATED WITH AUTISM

**Rationale:** Autism presents beyond social communication and sensory differences. Understanding associated concepts prevents misinterpretation of behaviours and supports neurodiversity-affirming responses. Recognising these patterns helps caregivers and professionals respond with clarity, respect, and appropriate support for individuals on the autism spectrum.

**Learning Outcome:** Trainees and participants will define and identify common autism-associated concepts and apply supportive strategies in home, school, and community settings.

### 1. Communication & Language Patterns

**Echolalia**

- Repeating words or phrases immediately or after a delay
- Can serve communicative functions: requesting, processing, self-regulation, or practising language

**Palilalia**

- Involuntary repetition of one's own words or phrases
- Often linked to self-soothing, anxiety, or neurological processing

### 2. Attention & Cognitive Processing Styles

**Monotropism**

- The tendency to focus deeply on a small number of interests while filtering out other input
- Explains intense concentration and difficulty shifting attention — it is a cognitive style, not defiance

**Hyperfixation**

- Intense, sustained focus on a specific subject or item
- Leads to deep knowledge and flow states, but can make transitions challenging

**Executive Dysfunction**

- Difficulty with planning, organisation, time management, and task initiation
- Not laziness — reflects differences in brain wiring for self-management

### 3. Emotional & Self-Awareness Differences

**Alexithymia**

- Difficulty identifying and describing one's own emotions
- May present as shutdowns or behaviour instead of verbal expression of feelings

**Masking / Camouflaging**

- Consciously suppressing traits associated with autism to fit in socially
- Often leads to exhaustion, anxiety, and mental health challenges — recognising masking is critical for authentic support

### 4. Sensory & Safety Related Concepts

**Hypersensitivity**

- Over-responsiveness to sensory input causing pain, anxiety, or avoidance

**Hyposensitivity**

- Under-responsiveness to sensory input, leading to sensory seeking or injury risk

**Sensory Overload**

- When the brain cannot process all sensory input, leading to meltdown or shutdown

**Nociception**

- Altered pain processing — some individuals on the autism spectrum may not feel pain, others may feel it intensely — always take pain reports seriously

**Elopement**

- Running or wandering away from safe spaces due to distress, curiosity, or sensory seeking
- Requires safety planning and understanding the 'why' behind the behaviour

### 5. Developmental Considerations

**Regression**

- Loss of previously acquired skills, often in language or social behaviour
- Can be triggered by illness, stress, trauma, or neurological changes — requires compassionate support and prompt assessment
